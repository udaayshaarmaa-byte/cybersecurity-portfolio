# Ethics in Cybersecurity



---

## What Are Security Ethics?

**Security ethics** = guidelines for making appropriate decisions as a security professional.

> Core rule: just because you *can* do something doesn't mean you *should*.
> Example: You have access to payroll data and could give yourself a raise — but that's an abuse of privilege.

---

## Part 1 — Three Core Ethical Principles

### 1. Confidentiality

- Security professionals handle **PII**, **SPII**, and proprietary data
- Ethical duty: keep that information confidential and safe — even from trusted coworkers
- Access privilege must never be abused

**Scenario:** Granting a coworker system access outside of properly documented channels "to help them out."
**Consequence:** Reprimands, loss of professional reputation, and legal consequences — for both parties.

---

### 2. Privacy Protection

- Safeguarding personal information from unauthorized use
- Obligation: follow your organization's policies — no case-by-case exceptions, even for managers

**Scenario:** Manager emails after hours requesting a colleague's personal phone number because they "can't access the database right now."
**Consequence:** Still a violation. Employee data stays in the secure database — no exceptions, no alternate formats.

---

### 3. Laws

- Laws = rules recognized by a community and enforced by a governing entity
- Negligence — not just deliberate action — can trigger compliance breaches and legal liability

**Scenario:** Hospital staff member leaves PII/SPII files unsupervised on a desk before a meeting. Files go missing.
**Consequence:** Multiple compliance regulations violated. Negligence = unethical AND illegal.

## Part 2 — Counterattacks and Ethical Frameworks

### US Standpoint on Counterattacks

| Item | Detail |
|------|--------|
| Relevant laws | Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) 1986, Cybersecurity Information Sharing Act (CISA) 2015 |
| Counterattacking classified as | **Vigilantism** — illegal for civilians |
| Risk | Further escalation, more damage, potential international incident |
| State-sponsored hacktivists | Counterattack = serious international implications |
| Who can counterattack | Approved federal government / military personnel only |

> **Verdict: Defend only. Counterattacks are illegal for all others.**

---

### International Standpoint — ICJ

The **International Court of Justice (ICJ)** states a counterattack may be lawful only if **all 4 conditions** are met:

| # | Condition |
|---|-----------|
| 01 | Only affects the party that attacked first — no collateral damage |
| 02 | Is a direct communication asking the initial attacker to stop |
| 03 | Does not escalate the situation |
| 04 | Effects can be fully reversed |

> Even when conditions are met, parameters are hard to measure and outcomes difficult to control.
> Reference: **Tallinn Manual** (updated guidance on international cyber law scenarios)

---

### Why Organizations Don't Counterattack

- Parameters are hard to verify in real time
- Negative outcomes are difficult to control
- Generally leads to a worse outcome, especially without expert experience
- Legal uncertainty across jurisdictions

## Ethical Principles and Methodologies (Expanded)

Because counterattacks are generally illegal or disapproved of, the security field relies on **frameworks and controls** — such as the CIA triad — to address confidentiality, privacy, and legal obligations.

### Confidentiality (expanded)
- Only authorized users can access specific assets or data
- Requires a high level of respect for privacy
- Key controls: access control, least privilege, no unauthorized sharing

### Privacy Protection (expanded)

| Term | Definition | Examples |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **PII** | Any info used to infer an individual's identity | Name, phone number |
| **SPII** | PII with stricter handling guidelines | SSN, credit card numbers |

Security professional obligations:
- Secure private information
- Identify security vulnerabilities
- Manage organizational risks
- Align security with business goals

### Laws (expanded) — Analyst Obligations

1. **Remain unbiased, honest, and responsible** — highest respect for the law, no personal exceptions
2. **Be transparent, just, and evidence-based** — rely on documented facts, not assumptions
3. **Stay consistently invested** — ethical issues require full engagement, not shortcuts
4. **Stay informed and advance your skills** — continuous learning is an ethical obligation

## Real-World Example — HIPAA

**Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA)** — US federal law protecting **PHI (Protected Health Information)**

- Patient information cannot be shared without consent
- If a breach exposes health data → security professionals must ensure the organization notifies affected patients
- Fulfilling both the **legal** and **ethical** obligation

---

## Key Takeaway

> Security teams are entrusted with greater access than other employees.
> That privilege must be respected at all times.
> When facing ambiguous situations → always adhere to **policies and procedures**, not personal judgment.
> Technology and attacker tactics evolve constantly — **strong ethics keep your decisions grounded**.

## Australian Market Context

| Framework / Law | Relevance |
|----------------|-----------|
| **Privacy Act 1988** | Australia's core personal data protection law |
| **Australian Privacy Principles (APPs)** | 13 principles defining lawful handling of PII and SPII |
| **Notifiable Data Breaches (NDB) Scheme** | Mandatory breach notification when PII is lost — mirrors HIPAA obligation |
| **Cybercrime Act 2001 (Cth)** | Australia's equivalent of CFAA — counterattacks similarly illegal for civilians |
| **ASD Essential Eight** | Access control maturity levels directly address confidentiality and privilege abuse |
